Understanding Ethiopia's Merchant Payment Landscape from the Ground Up

Merchant Payments Observatory · Wave 1 · n = 2,724 micro-enterprises

A nationally representative survey of micro-enterprises across urban and rural Ethiopia, examining the full arc of merchant digital payment readiness — from formal registration and infrastructure access through to adoption patterns, provider concentration, and the persistent barriers keeping merchants cash-based.

About the Observatory

The Merchant Payments Observatory is a multi-country research initiative designed to generate high-frequency, nationally representative evidence on merchant payment behavior. Operating across Pakistan, Nigeria, and Ethiopia, the Observatory combines depth, scale, and longitudinal design to move beyond adoption metrics.

The study tracks changes over time in merchant behavior, barriers, and preferences through panel data; measures digital payment readiness, adoption, and usage among formal and informal MSMEs; surfaces gender-differentiated patterns in access and adoption; and identifies persistent and emerging constraints — including infrastructure, cost, attitudes, and capability barriers — to galvanize policy, regulatory, and market interventions.

Methodology & Sample

The survey targets micro-enterprises — those with fewer than 5 employees — which represent 97% of the sample and the vast majority of Ethiopia's formal and informal merchant landscape. A total of 2,724 enterprises were enumerated, weighted to reflect population distributions across enterprise size and urban-rural breakdown.

Data collection captured both cash-only and digitally active merchants to provide a complete market view. The longitudinal design supports repeated survey waves to track adoption trends and behavioral shifts over time.

Key Findings

Ethiopia's merchants are transacting digitally, but on a narrow base — heavily concentrated in one provider, confined to personal accounts, and largely disconnected from the wider financial system.

  • 01

    Demographic Skew

    The market is overwhelmingly male-led and driven by youth, defining the core target for digital adoption programmes. Female-owned enterprises show a rising presence in urban areas, with 50.3% female ownership recorded in certain urban segments.

  • 02

    Bank-Led Concentration

    CBE commands a near-monopoly with an 83% primary provider share and 98% of mobile banking users, creating a single point of concentration risk for Ethiopia's merchant payment infrastructure.

  • 03

    The Maturity Paradox

    64% of businesses have operated for over 5 years, yet remain structurally informal — with 99% still relying on personal rather than business accounts, limiting providers' ability to distinguish commercial from personal transactions.

  • 04

    The Rural Bridge

    While urban areas are bank-led, E-Birr (Telebirr) dominates rural markets with 89% penetration, filling infrastructure gaps where mobile money acts as the primary financial access point.

  • 05

    Critical Infrastructure Gaps

    National ID ownership (61%) and reliable electricity access (with over two-thirds reporting daily or weekly outages) act as a hard ceiling for formal financial onboarding and sustained digital payment use.

  • 06

    Unmet Credit Demand

    45% of micro-enterprises need credit, but only 11% borrow formally. Heavy reliance on trust-based lending and informal credit sources represents a substantial opportunity for digital lending products.
